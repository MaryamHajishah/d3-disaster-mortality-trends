A data story · Natural disasters, 1900–2025

The deadliest age of natural disasters is behind us.

Since 1900 the number of recorded disasters has grown more than fortyfold, and it is easy to assume the danger grew with it. The record shows the opposite: the chance of dying in a disaster fell by over 90 percent. This is the story of how that happened, and of the one number now moving the wrong way.

01 · What you'd expect

More disasters of every kind

EM-DAT recorded 79 natural disasters in the 1900s. In the 2010s it recorded 3,647, a 46-fold increase, with storms and floods accounting for most of the growth. If it feels like the news brings a new disaster every week, this chart agrees with you.

So more people must be dying than ever. Right?

Part of this climb is bookkeeping, not weather: systematic global reporting only took shape after the 1960s, so the early decades miss many smaller events.

02 · The surprise

Deaths went the other way

In the 1900s, disasters killed about 9 of every 100,000 people alive. In the 2020s so far, that figure is below 0.6. While recorded disasters multiplied by 46, the risk of dying in one fell by more than 90 percent.

Almost nobody knows this. It may be the least reported success story of the last hundred years.

Each point is a decade average. The 2020s point covers 2020 to 2025 only.

02 · The surprise

The old killer was hunger

The towering bars early in the century are not collapsing buildings. They are droughts that turned into famines. The Chinese droughts of the 1920s and the 1960s each starved millions of people; a handful of events like these dominate the entire historical death toll.

02 · The surprise

And hunger is the problem we solved

Follow the drought layer alone: from millions of deaths per decade to a few thousand. Global food trade, weather forecasting and famine early-warning systems ended mass starvation from drought.

None of that was automatic. It was built, piece by piece, over decades. The collapse in disaster deaths is an achievement, not a coincidence.

03 · The new threat

One line is rising

Heat and cold waves are the exception to everything above. They killed more people in the first six years of the 2020s than in the entire 2010s, and much of that toll landed in wealthy countries that considered themselves safe. The European heat waves of 2003 and 2022 sit inside this line.

The 2020s bar is a six-year count and will grow before the decade ends.

03 · The new threat

Progress, seen from above

Press play and watch the century sweep by: the catastrophic dark patches of the famine decades appear, move, and then vanish. What remains today is a wide scatter of far lower rates. Not every country moves down, and recent upticks in wealthy regions come mostly from heat, but the extremes of the early century are gone.

Methodology

How this was built

Where the numbers come from, what was changed on the way, and where you should be skeptical.

Data sources

All figures come from EM-DAT, the international disaster database maintained by CRED at UCLouvain, retrieved as CSV downloads through Our World in Data.

    Cleaning and imputation

    • Yearly records were grouped into decades, because single years are dominated by whichever big event happened to fall in them.
    • Aggregate rows (World, continents, income groups) were separated from country rows; only countries with a standard ISO 3166 code appear on the map.
    • Missing values stay missing. They render as "no data" and were never filled in, interpolated or guessed.

    Processing pipeline

    1. Download the raw CSV and its metadata for each indicator from the Our World in Data API.
    2. Clean and aggregate in a documented pandas notebook, preprocessing/data_preprocessing.ipynb.
    3. Write one small JSON file per chart into data/.
    4. The page loads those files directly with D3. There is no server and no database.

    Limitations

    • EM-DAT's coverage improved a great deal after the 1980s. Early decades undercount smaller events, which inflates the apparent rise in disaster counts.
    • The 2020s are incomplete (2020 to 2025). Every chart flags this decade as partial.
    • Historical famine deaths are estimates. Numbers in the millions carry wide uncertainty, even though the overall pattern is not in doubt.
    • On the map, one catastrophic event can dominate a country's decade average. A dark country is not always a chronically dangerous one.

    Anything before 1980 deserves extra caution. The story's core claim, the fall in death rates, is large enough to survive these caveats.
